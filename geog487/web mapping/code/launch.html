Climate Change and Water Scarcity in Lethbridge, Alberta

Community Context

The City of Lethbridge is a growing community in southern Alberta, with a population of 98,406 in 2021 and a projected population of 115,000 by 2026 ("Statistics Canada", 2023; "World Population Review", n.d.). The city’s economy was traditionally based on agriculture, but has diversified into a major hub for southern Alberta’s industrial and commercial activities in recent years as well ("Kinsmen Club", n.d.). Lethbridge spans a total area of 127.19 km², divided by the Oldman River, and annually experiences an average maximum and minimum temperature of 12.8°C and −1.1°C, respectively ("Kinsmen Club", n.d.). Annual precipitation averages 380.2mm, with around 264 dry days per year, making Lethbridge the 11th driest city in Canada ("Kinsmen Club", n.d.).

Water scarcity is a large issue in Lethbridge. The city depends on the Oldman River and its reservoir system for municipal water supply (“Spring”, 2025). The three main reservoirs that feed the Oldman watershed are the Oldman Reservoir, the St. Mary Reservoir, and the Waterton Reservoir (“Spring”, 2025). In recent years, the region has experienced below-average mountain snowpack and precipitation, largely reducing runoff into the Oldman River system (“Spring”, 2025). Consequently, the Oldman Reservoir (the main reservoir feeding Lethbridge) was only approximately 65% full in 2025 (“Spring”, 2025). The entire Oldman River Basin has also been provincially designated as Drought Stage 3: Severe Drought in recent years, and Lethbridge has implemented voluntary water restrictions to reduce water waste (“Spring”, 2025). In the Prairie provinces, water scarcity is a major issue due to high agricultural irrigation demands (“Dryden”, 2021), and Southern Alberta is particularly dry, with Lethbridge receiving about 330 days of sunlight per year (“Location”, 2025). Across the province, 100% of available water sources have been allocated, creating vulnerability for both municipalities and industries (“Dryden”, 2021).

In the South Saskatchewan River Watershed, which includes the Oldman River Basin, rising temperatures are directly intensifying water scarcity. Warmer conditions can increase evapotranspiration rates and water demand from both natural ecosystems and agriculture (Mousavi, 2024a). These temperatures also cause snow events to occur later and melt during winter, reducing the quantity of snowmelt entering the reservoirs Lethbridge relies on (Mousavi, 2024a). Climate change is projected to greatly increase drought frequency and severity in the coming years, largely due to reduced precipitation and increased evaporation (Mousavi, 2024b). The locations and extent of the area most affected by drought will also change, shifting further east from the Rocky Mountains and covering ~76% more land by the end of the century (Mousavi, 2024b).

Overall, climate change is a primary cause of water scarcity in Lethbridge. This project will illustrate this relationship by presenting a time series comparison of key climate factors with indicators of water scarcity over the last decade, with a focus on the climate change factors of surface temperature, precipitation levels, and drought conditions. Temporally visualizing these trends in Lethbridge's reservoirs will help demonstrate how climate change impacts one of the most critical facets of the city’s water distribution system.
